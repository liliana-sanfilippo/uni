\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection*{What is Knowledge Representation?}
\defin{kr}

The core problem with KR is that the real world with its complexity, ambiguity and dynamism can never be fully represented in a computer-based system.
Nevertheless, intelligent systems must be able to function effectively using partial, incomplete and sometimes contradictory information.
\cite{folien1}

\subsection*{Why is Knowledge Representation needed?}

\subsubsection*{The problem of data meaninglessness}
To understand the need for KR, let’s consider a practical scenario:
\begin{bsp}

    A student database.

    A hypothetical database contains the following entries:

    \begin{table}[H]
        \centering
        \begin{tabular}{lllll}
            \textbf{Name} & \textbf{Major} & \textbf{CGPA} & \textbf{Research}   & \ldots \\
            Alice Smith   & CS             & 3.8           & Genomics            & \ldots \\
            Bob John      & Biology        & 3.8           & Oncology            & \ldots \\
            Carol Li      & Zoology        & 3.7           & Animal colonisation & \ldots
        \end{tabular}
        \label{tab:first-example}
    \end{table}

    Without knowledge representation, this data is disjointed.
    The entries exist in isolation across thousands of files.
    A database query system can only perform exact keyword matches:

    \begin{itemize}
        \item Query 1: ``Show me all AI researchers'' → No results (not explicitly mentioned in the data)
        \item Query 2: ``Which students could work on this patent?'' → No answer possible
        \item Query 3: ``What are the emerging trends?'' → No analysis possible
    \end{itemize}

\end{bsp}

Data is meaningless without context and relationships.
It does not allow for intelligent reasoning or deeper analysis.

\subsection*{What knowledge representation makes possible}

With knowledge representation, we can structure the data and transfer isolated records into interconnected,
queryable knowledge using:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \glspl{ontology}: Explicitly specify which concepts exist (student, researcher, field of research)
    and how they are categorised.
    \item Knowledge graphs: Link entities together: Alice → researcher → AI
\end{itemize}

This allows for semantic searches, automatic relationship discovery and reasoning across all connection.


\begin{bsp}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Semantic search
        \begin{itemize}
            \item ``Find students researching machine learning?''
            \item ``Show me all AI researchers'': The system can use transitive relationships to find relevant students.
            \item ``Which students are a good fit for this patent?'': The system can match skills and areas of interest.
        \end{itemize}
        \item Automatic relationship discovery
        \begin{itemize}
            \item ``What are emerging research trends?''
            \item Recognising patterns across all data
            \item Identifying emerging trends through interconnected information
        \end{itemize}
    \end{itemize}

\end{bsp}

\begin{table}[H]
    \begin{tabular}{p{5cm}p{5cm}p{5cm}}
        \Gls{data}      & \Gls{information}                                 & \Gls{knowledge}                   \\
        Raw, unprocessed facts & Structured and contextualised data & Information combined with experience and
        reasoning rules \\
        e.g.\ CGPA: 3.8 & e.g.\ Alice has a CGPA of 3.8 in Computer Science & e.g.\ Alice could work on this AI
        patent because she has a strong foundation in computer science and is interested in genomics
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Data vs. Information vs. Knowledge }\label{tab:data-information-knowledge}
\end{table}

KR enables machines to make informed decisions with fewer rules.

\subsection*{KR Perspective}

\paragraph{Traditional ML} systems follow a black-box approach which means the decision-making process is not transparent and the reasoning behind predictions is not accessible.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Generalisation: Requires continuous retraining with new data
    \item Explainability: Difficult or impossible to explain decisions
    \item Data flexibility: Highly dependent on large amounts of training data
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Knowledge-Based AI} utilises structured, \gls{explicit knowledge}.

\defin{explicit knowledge}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Generalization: explicit knowledge transfers to new problems without retraining
    from scratch\cite{hogan2021}
    \item Explainability: encoded knowledge enables transparent reasoning and human-understandable decisions\cite{cao2024}
    \item Data flexibility: Modifiable
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Challenges in Knowledge Representation}
Knowledge representation involves designing formal structures that allow computers to store, interpret, and reason
about information and every design decision has advantages and caveats.

\paragraph{Choosing the representation} is one of the design challenges.
\Glspl{semantic-network} are intuitive and visually understandable but have limited inference. \Gls{fol} on the other
hand is
expressive, but has computationally costly reasoning.

\paragraph{Desired properties} can conflict.
Systems should ideally be expressive enough to capture rich real-world knowledge while remaining efficient enough to reason in practical time.
Tradeoffs are between expressiveness and tractability, and Human readability and machine efficiency.

\paragraph{Application-driven constraints} arise from, different domains requiring different forms of representation.
E.g.\ In natural language processing (NLP), systems must handle ambiguity and context.
In medical diagnosis, representations must manage uncertainty, causal relationships, and strict correctness
requirements.
In question answering, systems need fast retrieval combined with reasoning over large amounts of structured and unstructured data

\paragraph{The core idea of Knowledge Representation} is not just a data structure.
It plays multiple roles
simultaneously and those roles can conflict with each other:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Surrogate for the world: Acts as a substitute for real world objects and relationships
    \item Ontological commitments: Defines what exists and how things are categorized
    \item Theory of reasoning: Provides foundation for logical inference and deduction
    \item Medium for efficient computation: Enables performant computational processing
    \item Medium of Human expression: Allows humans to express knowledge in understandable ways
\end{itemize}

\section{Distinguishing Features of KR Systems}\label{sec:distinguishing-features-of-kr-systems}
Unlike databases (retrieval) or programs (execution), knowledge bases reuse the same facts in multiple ways:

\subsection*{Inference (rule-based reasoning):}
Facts are used to derive new facts by applying rules.
\begin{bsp}
    We remember Dr. Dokter from fig. \ref{fig:drdr}.
    \begin{center}
        IF fever $\land$ cough $\rightarrow$ possible infection
    \end{center}
    $\Rightarrow$ Diagnostic conclusion
\end{bsp}

It has multiple subtypes:
\defin{loginf}
\begin{bsp}
    $\ $
    \begin{center}
        IF fever $\rightarrow$ infection \\
        fever $\Rightarrow$ infection
    \end{center}
\end{bsp}

\defin{specprod}

\begin{bsp}
    Ward A $\rightarrow$ Ward B $\rightarrow$ ICU $\Rightarrow$ Shortest path
\end{bsp}

\defin{prefinf}

\begin{bsp}
    $\ $
    \begin{center}
        Birds fly \\
        Tweety is a bird $\Rightarrow$ Tweety flies \\ (except penguins)
    \end{center}
\end{bsp}

\defin{indinf}

\begin{bsp}
    $\ $
    \begin{center}
        Data $\Rightarrow$ Pattern \\
        Symptoms $\Rightarrow$ Diagnosis model
    \end{center}
\end{bsp}

\defin{abdinf}

\begin{bsp}
    $\ $
    \begin{center}
        Observed: fever + cough \\
        $\Rightarrow$ Best explanation: pneumonia
    \end{center}
\end{bsp}

\subsection*{Access (Direct fact retrieval via queries):}
Facts are retrieved directly, much like in a database.
\begin{bsp}
    Query: ``Which patients have fever?'' $\Rightarrow$ Retrieve patient X
\end{bsp}

\subsection*{Matching (Similarity-based reasoning):} Facts are used to identify similar patterns and perform case-based reasoning.

\begin{bsp}
    $\ $
    \begin{center}
        X $\sim$ Patient Y $\Rightarrow$ Case-based reasoning
    \end{center}
\end{bsp}

\subsection*{Design implications}
When designing a system, we must choose which inference mechanisms to support (e.g., shortest-path search vs.
rule-based reasoning).
Of course, there are trade-offs between expressiveness and efficiency.
And different applications require different inference types, e.g.\ the Pac-Man game (example~\ref{bsp:pacman}) needs
heuristic inference for real-time decisions goals.

%Wie man mit Incompleteness umgeht



\begin{obs}
    \label{obs:incompleteness}
    Knowledge bases cannot completely describe the world they model
    (except artificial microworlds)

    \paragraph{Consequences} exist for operations, reasoning and design methodology.
\end{obs}

\subsubsection*{Consequences for Operations}
\begin{imp}
    \label{imp:worlds} Regarding observation \autoref{obs:incompleteness} \\
    There are different answers to existence queries depending on assumptions. \\
    We can assume a closed world, where everything not explicitly stated in the knowledge base is assumed FALSE or
    an open world, where we simply cannot conclude anything if it is not explicitly stated in the knowledge base.
\end{imp}



\begin{bsp}
    To definition \autoref{ef:cwa} - Closed World Assumption \\
    \begin{center}
        Knowledge Base: Alice (New York), Bob (London), Carol (Paris) \\
        Query: ``Is there a person in Toronto?'' \\
        CWA Answer: NO (because Toronto is not mentioned in the KB) \\
        Reasoning: If someone lived in Toronto, the KB would state it.
    \end{center}
\end{bsp}

\begin{bsp}
    To definition \autoref{def:owa} - Open World Assumption \\
    \begin{center}
        Knowledge Base: Alice (New York), Bob (London), Carol (Paris) \\
        Query: ``Is there a person in Toronto?'' \\
        OWA Answer: Unknown (because Toronto is not mentioned, but also not excluded) \\
        Reasoning: The KB does not mention Toronto, but that does not mean no one lives there.
    \end{center}
\end{bsp}
The choice between CWA and OWA fundamentally influences system behavior and must be made during the design phase.

\subsubsection*{Consequences for Reasoning}
Incompleteness requires special reasoning strategies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Assumption of Defaults: In the absence of information, make reasonable assumptions
    \item Probabilistic Reasoning: Explicitly model uncertainties
    \item Constraint Propagation: Use constraints to reduce search spaces
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Consequences for Design Methodology}
Unlike machine learning, where knowledge is implicit in model parameters and retraining is required, in KGs knowledge is explicit and can be extended, revised, and corrected without retraining the entire model.

This distinction is important for practical systems where expert knowledge needs to be continuously updated without
expensive computational overhead.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{p{5cm}p{5cm}p{5cm}}
        \textbf{Programming} & \textbf{Knowledge Representation} & \textbf{Machine Learning} \\
        ``Once and for all'' complete specification.
        Code is written and then ``frozen'' & Continuous, incremental
        development.
        The knowledge base is iteratively extended, revised, and refined
        &
        Knowledge is implicitly encoded in model parameters and requires retraining when incomplete
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Differences in knowledge/information acquisition and handling.}\label{tab:table}
\end{table}

\section{Future Directions}\label{sec:future-directions}

\subsection*{Multi-hop Reasoning (Complex Reasoning)}
Many questions require multiple reasoning steps across relationships.
\begin{bsp}
    Query: ``What medicine treats diseases caused by virus X?'' \\
    Requires: \\
    medicine --[treats]--> disease --[caused\_by]--> virus \\
    Path: \\
    Starting from virus\_X: \\
    virus\_X --[causes]--> disease\_Y \\
    disease\_Y --[treated\_by]--> medicine\_Z \\
    Result: medicine\_Z
\end{bsp}
Simple embedding methods struggle with this multi-step logical chain, so complex multi-hop relations require dedicated
design. \\
\textbf{Promising Directions:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Recurrent Relational Path Encoding:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Methods like DeepPath use LSTM networks
        \item The LSTM ``encodes'' a path like: Person $\rightarrow$ LivesIn $\rightarrow$ City $\rightarrow$ LocatedIn $\rightarrow$ Country
        \item This encoded path representation can then be used for reasoning
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{GNN-based Message Passing:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) aggregate information from neighbors
        \item Multi-hop reasoning through iterative message passing
        \item Each ``hop'' collects information from the next node
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Reinforcement Learning-based Path Finding:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item An agent learns to navigate through the knowledge graph
        \item Example agent path: ``Marie Curie $\rightarrow$ BornIn $\rightarrow$ Ulm $\rightarrow$ \dots $\rightarrow$ Paris''
        \item The agent receives rewards when reaching the correct target
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\defin{one-hop}
\defin{multi-hop}

\begin{bsp}
    To definitions \autoref{def:one-hop} and \autoref{def:multi-hop} - Simple vs.\ Multi-hop Reasoning \\
    A simple 1-hop query can be expressed as:
    \[
        \text{Einstein} \rightarrow \text{worked\_at} \rightarrow \text{Princeton}
    \]

    A more complex multi-hop query is:
    \emph{Where did Einstein's doctoral advisor teach?}

    This requires chaining relations:
    \[
        \text{Einstein} \rightarrow \text{advised\_by} \rightarrow \text{Kleiner} \rightarrow \text{worked\_at} \rightarrow \text{University of Zurich}
    \]

    This corresponds to a 2-hop reasoning process.
\end{bsp}
Structured knowledge provides commonsense observations and acts as relational inductive biases (assumptions about
the world). Meaning they provide such biases like: ``Some relations are more likely than others''. It also boosts
commonsense knowledge fusion between symbolic and semantic space. E.g.:
\begin{bsp}
    \textbf{Query:} \emph{Can a fish play piano?}

    \textbf{Without Knowledge:}
    A machine learning model could predict this based on training data, but provide no reasoning.

    \textbf{With Knowledge:}

    \begin{align*}
        \text{Fish} &\rightarrow \text{hasBodyPart} \rightarrow \text{Fins} \quad (\text{not Hands}) \\
        \text{PlayingPiano} &\rightarrow \text{requires} \rightarrow \text{Hands}
    \end{align*}

    \textbf{Symbolic Rule:}
    If $X \rightarrow \text{hasBodyPart} \rightarrow p$ and $\text{action} \rightarrow \text{requires} \rightarrow p$,
    then $X \rightarrow \text{can} \rightarrow \text{action}$.

    \textbf{Application:}
    \begin{align*}
        \text{Fish} &\rightarrow \text{hasBodyPart} \rightarrow \text{Fins} \\
        \text{PlayingPiano} &\rightarrow \text{requires} \rightarrow \text{Hands} \\
        \text{Fins} &\neq \text{Hands}
    \end{align*}

    \textbf{Conclusion:}
    \[
        \text{Fish} \rightarrow \text{cannot} \rightarrow \text{PlayPiano}
    \]

    \textbf{Answer with Reasoning:}
    No, a fish cannot play piano because it lacks hands.
\end{bsp}
Commonsense knowledge provides strong priors, enabling systems to function with less training data.

\subsection*{Unified Framework}
Many KR embedding methods are mathematically equivalent under transformation.
Actually, several representation learning models are verified as equivalent.
\begin{bsp}
    \textbf{Example: Unifying Knowledge Graph Embedding Models}
    \begin{align*}
        \text{HolE:} \quad & h \star r \quad \text{(circular correlation)} \\
        \text{ComplEx:} \quad & \text{complex-valued embeddings} \\
        \text{DistMult:} \quad & \text{real-valued symmetric embeddings}
    \end{align*}

    These can be understood as special cases of a more general framework.

    \medskip

    \textbf{ANALOGY Framework:}

    The ANALOGY framework unifies these approaches via analogical inference:
    \[
        h + r \approx t
    \]

    \noindent
    where an entity $h$ combined with a relation $r$ approximates the target entity $t$.

    \textbf{Interpretation:}
    Different models correspond to different ways of representing this analogy.
\end{bsp}

\subsection*{Interpretability}
Modern neural-KR systems (KG embedding models) work well, but it's unclear \textit{why}.

\begin{bsp}
    \textbf{Example: Lack of Interpretability in Neural Models} \\
    \textbf{Prediction:}
    Neural model predicts:
    \[
        \text{Aspirin} \rightarrow \text{treats} \rightarrow \text{Headache}
    \]

    \textbf{Question:}
    Why? Which reasoning path?

    \textbf{Reasoning:}
    Just learned embedding similarity.
\end{bsp}
There are different ways this could habe been reasoned:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Path-based explanation:} \\
    Aspirin $\rightarrow$ type $\rightarrow$ Painkiller $\rightarrow$ treats $\rightarrow$ Pain $\rightarrow$ symptom\_of $\rightarrow$ Headache

    \item \textbf{Attention visualization:} \\
    Model focuses on ``anti-inflammatory'' and ``pain-relief'' relations

    \item \textbf{Rule extraction:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item IF drug $\rightarrow$ has\_property $\rightarrow$ analgesic AND disease $\rightarrow$ has\_symptom $\rightarrow$ pain
        \item THEN drug $\rightarrow$ treats $\rightarrow$ disease
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
And different applications:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Medical diagnosis:} \\
    Doctor needs to see reasoning path before trusting AI recommendation

    \item \textbf{Legal applications:} \\
    Must explain why system reached a conclusion
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Knowledge Aggregation}
Knowledge aggregation is the core of knowledge-aware applications. Many approaches rely on neural architectures. NLP
has been significantly improved by large-scale pre-training. A key challenge remains the efficient and interpretable
aggregation of knowledge.
\begin{bsp}
    \textbf{Query:}
    Who directed Inception?

    \textbf{Knowledge neighbors:}
    \[
        [\text{Nolan}, \text{Warner Bros}, 2010, \text{Sci-Fi}, \text{DiCaprio}]
    \]

    \textbf{Attention weights:}
    \[
        \text{director} = 0.8, \quad \text{actor} = 0.15, \quad \text{studio} = 0.05
    \]

    \textbf{Interpretation:}
    The model focuses on the most relevant relation (director).
\end{bsp}

\subsection*{Automatic Construction and Dynamics}
Knowledge graphs are currently mostly manually constructed or extracted from structured sources (e.g. Wikipedia),
which is very labor-intensive.

We would need automatic construction from large-scale unstructured content, but there are several challanges:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Multimodality: Information can be in text, images, video

    \item Heterogeneity: Different sources use different schemas

    \item Large-scale: Billions of documents

\end{itemize}
Additionally, \glspl{kg} should be dynamic, as real-world facts change over time.
\begin{bsp}
    \textbf{Static representation:}
    \[
        (\text{Pluto}, \text{classified\_as}, \text{Planet})
    \]

    \textbf{Dynamic representation:}
    \begin{align*}
    (\text{Pluto}, \text{classified\_as}, \text{Planet}, [1930\text{-}2006])
        \\
        (\text{Pluto}, \text{classified\_as}, \text{Dwarf\_Planet}, [2006\text{-present}])
    \end{align*}

    \textbf{Requirement:}
    System must track temporal validity of facts.
\end{bsp}
